Restricting to CODIS core loci but keeping information in both columns¶

Just like last time, we will restrict to the CODIS loci. We'll exclude 'AMEL' because it doesn't have quantitative alleles. We'll also keep 'Group' because it might be useful for further analysis later.

However, unlike last time, we'll include the information from both XXXX and XXXX.1 columns. There is more than one way to do this, so feel free to do it your way.

Q2: Create a new table, CODIS_profiles_new, with the CODIS loci and 'Group', but this time include both columns. For example the 'TPOX' column in CODIS_profiles_new will contain the values in both 'TPOX' and 'TPOX.1' in the old CODIS_profiles. If you want to use my code, pasted below, you'll need to make a small modification for it to work.

# Append a copy of the 'Group' column
# to the original column
group=raw_profiles.column('Group')
new_group=np.array(list(group)*2)
# when you multiply a list by k, it concatenates
# itself with itself k-1 times

# initialize a new table with the column new_group
CODIS_profiles_new=Table()
CODIS_profiles_new.append_column('Ethnicity', new_group)

# for the other columns, loop through the loci, concatenate
# XXXX and XXXX.1 columns, and append to table
CODIS_loci=['TPOX', 'D3S1358', 'FGA', 'D5S818', 
            'CSF1PO', 'D7S820', 'D8S1179', 'TH01', 'vWA',
            'D13S317', 'D16S539', 'D18S51', 'D21S11']
for l in CODIS_loci:
    col1=raw_profiles.column(l)
    col2=raw_profiles.column(l+'.1') # concatenate strings to get name of second column
    new_col=np.array(list(col1)+list(col2)) # concatenate lists, make into array
    CODIS_profiles_new.append_column(l, new_col)

# I just manually coded the above table into a list of tuples, one tuple for each STR locus.
# Each tuple lists the two alleles at the locus
fingerprint=[(11,12),(17,18),(22,25),(12,13),(12,12),(11,11),(13,15),(7,9),(16,19),(10,11)
            ,(12,12),(15,17),(31,33.2)]

prob=1 # intiate match prob
for l,a in zip(CODIS_loci, fingerprint):
    prob_alleles=1
    for i in a:
        # for each allele at locus l
        allele_index=np.where(CODIS_profiles_new.group(l).column(l)==i)[0][0]
        # determine the row index of allele i
        prob_alleles*=CODIS_profiles_new.group(l).row(allele_index)[1]/CODIS_profiles_new.num_rows
    prob*=prob_alleles
    print(l,a, prob_alleles)

The probability that the suspect is innocent given the DNA fingerprint matches is  0.5

Assuming that the suspect was selected uniformly at random from the entire US population, and that the CODIS dataset is representative of the DNA fingerprints in the US, the probability that the suspect is innocent, given that their DNA fingerprint matches the DNA belonging to the perpetrator is around $10^{-12}$, which most of us would consider to be the same as zero. In other words, on the basis of DNA evidence alone, there is strong support for guilt. However, the DNA evidence should be considered in combination with the other facts of a case, and this should be be emphasized when explaining to someone who is not as familiar with the assumptions underlying the calculation.

A lay person may not have much intuition for the numbers $10^{-21}$ and $10^{-12}$. Here's how I would try to explain it (and this is by no means the best way):

We've found that the probability that the suspect is innocent given that they match the perpetrator's DNA is virtually zero. However, this number is calculated under the assumption that each person's DNA fingerprint is generated (essentially) by drawing numbers out of a hat. In reality, our DNA fingerprints are strongly correlated with those of our relatives. In the extreme case of monozygotic (identical) twins, their DNA fingerprints are identical.

If it is possible that the suspect has an identical twin then the probability the suspect is innocent given they match the perpetrator's DNA increases from virtually zero to at least a half, in the absence of other evidence. Even in the less extreme case, if the suspect has close relatives who may have perpetrated the crime, closer scrutiny of the DNA sample collection and forensic analysis would be required to rule out the possibility that a relative didn't commit the crime.
